Thesis

My undergraduate thesis focuses on "Multimodal Corrosion Prediction in Reinforced Concrete Structures." I explore innovative ways to predict corrosion levels in concrete using machine learning techniques. Specifically, I use surface roughness, resistivity, and half-cell potential data to develop a predictive model for identifying corrosion in reinforced concrete, which can lead to more efficient monitoring and maintenance strategies in civil infrastructure.

Thesis

MULTIMODAL CORROSION PREDICTION IN REINFORCED CONCRETE USING SURFACE ROUGHNESS FROM IMAGE, RESISTIVITY AND HALF-CELL DATA

Corrosion Prediction Approach
  • The research aims to enhance corrosion prediction in reinforced concrete structures by integrating multiple data types: surface roughness from high-resolution images, electrochemical data from half-cell potential readings, and concrete resistivity measurements.
  • The surface roughness, extracted using advanced image processing techniques like Sobel edge detection, is used as an early indicator of corrosion damage, while electrochemical data provides a more direct assessment of corrosion risks.
Machine Learning Models

  • Various machine learning models were trained using the multimodal dataset to predict the percentage of corrosion in concrete structures. The models tested include:

    • Random Forest: Best performing model with an R2 score of 0.89, showing high accuracy in predicting corrosion.
    • Support Vector Machine (SVM): Achieved an R2 of 0.6758, showing moderate predictive accuracy.
    • K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN): An R2 score of 0.8459, performing well but not as accurately as Random Forest.
    • MLP Regressor (Multi-Layer Perceptron): The lowest performing model with an R2 of 0.6535.

  • The Random Forest model outperformed the others, showing that integrating diverse data types leads to better corrosion prediction accuracy.

Key Findings
  • Surface Resistivity was identified as the most significant feature for predicting corrosion. It strongly correlates with the ionic conductivity of the concrete, directly affecting the likelihood of corrosion
  • Surface Roughness (extracted from images) was found to provide valuable early-stage corrosion insights, even before visible damage, like cracks or spalling, appears. This makes surface roughness a useful tool for early detection.
  • The combination of both electrochemical data and image-derived features (surface roughness) significantly improved the model’s predictive capabilities compared to traditional methods, which rely solely on electrochemical readings.
Practical Implications
  • The model developed in the research offers a cost-effective, non-invasive, and early detection method for monitoring corrosion in concrete structures. By using a combination of imaging and electrochemical data, engineers can identify corrosion before visible damage occurs, enabling proactive maintenance.
  • This model can be integrated into existing infrastructure monitoring systems, making it possible to transition from reactive to predictive maintenance, which can ultimately help extend the service life of concrete structures and reduce the costs associated with repairs and replacements.
  • The research contributes to more efficient and accurate monitoring of reinforced concrete structures, promoting better safety and durability in civil infrastructure.
